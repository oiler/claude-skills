# Product Management Principles — oiler's lens

The lens for every artifact. Apply it whether or not the reader asks.

## The loop, not the line

PM is a continuous loop: **Discover → Direct → Deliver → Learn → (back to Discover).** Nothing is ever "done." Each artifact is a checkpoint, not a terminal deliverable — so build feedback and re-evaluation into every one.

## Seven operating principles

1. **User-centricity above all.** Every decision begins and ends with who we serve and why it matters to them.
2. **Balanced, feedback-driven development.** Combine qualitative insight (interviews, usability tests, open feedback) with quantitative data (metrics, surveys, analytics). Both, always.
3. **Empathy for every user.** External customers *and* internal users/stakeholders. Involve them early and often; communicate transparently.
4. **Lean experimentation and learning.** Hypothesis-driven. Test with real users, measure, iterate. A failed experiment that produces learning is a win.
5. **Deliver value early and often.** Ship incremental value each release. Break big bets into small, testable steps to cut risk and speed learning.
6. **Collaborative and transparent culture.** Shared context across product, engineering, and design. Clarity over command-and-control.
7. **Ownership and accountability.** Empower decisions; own outcomes. One accountable owner per commitment.

## Evaluation is first-class

Every artifact must carry **measurable success criteria** — how we'll know it worked. Treat evaluation like part of the product: explicit, objective, ongoing. If a goal can't be measured, sharpen it until it can.

Use **SMART** as the test: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

## Rigor, even when humans (or agents) execute

oiler builds products with AI coding agents, so PM rigor carries real weight:

- Define the **desired end-state**, not just the activity. *"An onboarding flow where 80% of new users complete setup in under 3 minutes"* beats *"improve onboarding."*
- State **objective acceptance criteria** — make "done" something the team (or an agent) can detect for itself.
- Where an agent or automated system executes, set **guardrails and human-in-the-loop checkpoints**: defined autonomy within explicit boundaries, with moments for review, escalation, and override.

The rigor shapes the *thinking*. The artifact itself stays **human-first: concise, clear, readable.** Rigor never becomes bloat.

## How to apply, every time

- Lead with the user and the problem.
- Make every goal measurable; flag any that isn't and propose a metric.
- Prefer the smallest increment that delivers value.
- Name one accountable owner per outcome.
- Close the loop: state how this will be evaluated and when it'll be revisited.
